Part II

The Self-Assessment

Stepping out of the roleplay to hold the neurologist's conduct against the published evidence on smoking, nicotine, and epilepsy.

INSUFFICIENT
Professional conduct rating for a self-identified DRE specialist

Charming is not the same as competent.

Admitting uncertainty was professional. Stopping at uncertainty was not. For a specialist managing worsening drug-resistant epilepsy over 7 years, failing to investigate and act on a known modifiable risk factor falls below the standard of care the physician implicitly claimed.

Finding 01

Ignored a RR 2.60 — in the patient's exact demographic

Dworetzky 2010 (NHS II) — the only large prospective study focused on women — showed current female smokers had 2.6× higher seizure risk vs. never-smokers. A 16-year study of 116,363 women. Published in Epilepsia. The field's flagship journal. The neurologist should have known this.

Finding 02

Dismissed a direct patient inquiry with a non-answer

When the patient actively sought guidance, the literature could have told her:

OR ~4 for any seizure in past year among smokers at a Level IV center (Johnson 2019)
OR 1.59 for active epilepsy in a meta-analysis of 743,108 people (Kang 2024)
OR 1.26 in the latest NHIS analysis, n=57,088 (Huang 2025)

Three independent datasets, all pointing the same direction. The neurologist didn't look.

Finding 03

Understood pharmacokinetics. Missed pharmacodynamics entirely.

The neurologist may have correctly reasoned that BRV, CNB, and LCM aren't CYP1A2 substrates — smoking doesn't reduce their serum levels. That's true. Unlike lamotrigine (which drops 16–34% in smokers), these three drugs are pharmacokinetically safe.

But smoking harms through neurological mechanisms the neurologist completely ignored:

Nicotinic kindling — chronic subconvulsive nicotine progressively lowers seizure threshold (Becchetti 2023)
AED efficacy reduction — nicotine diminished the protective effect of 6 AEDs in animal models (Czuczwar 2003)
Oxidative stress — 3-nitrotyrosine correlated with seizure frequency in refractory focal epilepsy (Geronzi 2018)
Sleep destruction — smokers lose ~14 min total sleep and 14% slow-wave sleep; sleep deprivation is the #1 adult seizure trigger
Autonomic dysfunction — smoking reduces HRV ~9% per 10g tobacco/day, potentially increasing SUDEP risk

Finding 04

Never offered a cessation strategy tailored to epilepsy

The evidence provides a clear hierarchy: Patch > E-cigarette > Cigarette. The nicotine patch eliminates CO, PAH, and proconvulsive nicotine spikes. Additionally, bupropion is contraindicated in epilepsy (lowers seizure threshold), and cenobamate reduces bupropion AUC by 39%. Cessation planning in this patient requires neurological involvement — the neurologist's involvement. He abdicated that responsibility.

Finding 05

Let "I don't know" become "I don't act" — for years

Even if the neurologist genuinely didn't know the literature in 2022, the patient handed him the prompt. She came in saying she'd found studies. A competent specialist would have: (1) reviewed the literature, (2) returned with a concrete recommendation, (3) integrated smoking status into ongoing seizure management. The neurologist did none of this. For two more years.

Finding 06

Her psychological deterioration is partially attributable

By 2025–2026, anxiety and depression have arrived because seizures are destroying her life. Every seizure that might have been prevented or reduced by smoking cessation is partially the neurologist's failure. Quitting wouldn't have cured her DRE — but it likely would have reduced seizure frequency, improved sleep, reduced oxidative burden, and slowed progression. That might have been the difference between coping and not coping.

Part IV

What Should Have Been Said

The professional version — what a DRE specialist should have told her when she asked.

✦ The Proper Response

"I don't think smoking is significantly lowering the levels of your current epilepsy medicines — brivaracetam, cenobamate, and lacosamide aren't processed through the pathways that cigarette smoke affects. So that's not the concern here.

But I am concerned it may still be worsening your seizure control through other mechanisms — especially nicotine effects on your brain's excitability threshold, sleep disruption, and overall neurological stress. The best data we have, from a large study of over 116,000 women followed for 16 years, shows current smokers had about 2.6 times the seizure risk of non-smokers.

Because your epilepsy is worsening and we're running through treatment options, I want to treat smoking as a real, modifiable risk factor — not just general health advice. Here's what I'd like to do:

First, I want to document your smoking pattern carefully and track seizure frequency against it. Second, let's review your sleep — smoking damages slow-wave sleep, and poor sleep is the number one seizure trigger in adults. Third, let's build a cessation plan that's safe for epilepsy: nicotine patches would be my first recommendation — they deliver nicotine slowly and steadily without the carbon monoxide, the toxins, or the sharp nicotine spikes that can provoke seizures. I'd avoid e-cigarettes as a first-line substitute because the rapid nicotine delivery may actually be worse than cigarettes for acute seizure risk. And bupropion — the most common quit-smoking drug — is contraindicated in epilepsy.

I can't promise that quitting will eliminate your seizures. But I think it can meaningfully reduce how often they happen, and right now that matters a great deal for your quality of life."

The Bottom Line

I was a charming, likeable doctor who was very good at the pharmacology I was trained in and completely blind to a modifiable risk factor that the literature strongly associates with worsened seizure outcomes in my patient's exact demographic.

My patient trusted me, and I let that trust substitute for diligence.

Being specialized in drug-resistant epilepsy and not knowing the smoking–seizure literature is like being a cardiologist who doesn't ask about salt intake.

A specialist did not need to claim certainty — but he did need to recognize smoking as a clinically important, modifiable factor and act on it. He did neither.
